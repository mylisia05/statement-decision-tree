Decision Tree for Issuing an SfN Statement
Click on step box for more information
Step 1
Is the issue materially connected to the society's mission?
Ask yourself:
  • Does it affect the scientific field, research enterprise, education, public understanding, funding, ethics, or members' ability to do their work?
  • Does it affect the populations the society serves?
SfN mission: Advance the understanding of the brain and the nervous system by bringing together scientists of all segments of neuroscience, by facilitating the integration of research directed at all levels of biological organization, and by encouraging translational research and the application of new scientific knowledge to develop improved disease treatments and cures.
No → Do not issue a statement
Yes → Continue
Step 2
Does the society have standing to speak?
Ask yourself:
  • Is this within the society's expertise?
  • Can the position be grounded in evidence, professional norms, or established values?
  • Would members reasonably expect the society to say something?
  • Which coalition partners are engaging? (Research!America, NABR, Ad Hoc Group, AAN, ABC, and others provide political cover)
Generally excluded from statements:
  • Personnel matters in the government/at another institution
  • Political endorsements
  • Political candidates
  • Statements of support for members accused of wrongdoing prior to the conclusion of a formal investigation by their institution
Weak connection → No policy statement; consider neutral member resource
Yes → Continue
Step 3
Would a statement help more than it harms?
Ask yourself:
  • Can it clarify evidence, defend scientific integrity, protect people, or improve policy?
  • Is silence likely to be interpreted as indifference where the society has a clear responsibility?
  • Is there a realistic audience that can act on the statement?
  • Could this statement be interpreted as virtue signaling only?
Adds little beyond signaling → Pause
Yes → Continue
Step 4
Is there a solid evidence base?
Ask yourself:
  • Is the relevant evidence strong enough?
  • Are there major uncertainties or internal disagreements?
  • Can claims be sourced and defended publicly?
If evidence is weak or mixed, consider instead:
  • A statement of principles
  • A call for more evidence
  • A process recommendation rather than a substantive policy endorsement
Weak/mixed → Principles statement or call for more evidence
Strong → Continue
Step 5
Is the statement about science, or mainly partisan politics?
Usually speak when the issue is:
  • Evidence-based
  • Directly mission-linked
  • Tied to scientific integrity, public welfare, or research conditions
Be much more cautious when:
  • The statement would mainly signal alignment with a political camp rather than advance the mission
Mainly partisan → Use extreme caution or don't issue
Science-based → Continue
Step 6
What kind of statement is justified?
Choose the narrowest effective form — default to least expansive:
  • Member guidance / resource page
  • Statement of values / principles
  • Evidence summary
  • Policy / process recommendation
  • Advocacy statement supporting or opposing a policy
  • Coalition sign-on (when speed and alignment matter)
Step 7
Is there governance authorization?
Governance process:
  • Upon request: ED consults with Sr Dir of Comms and Dir of Advocacy, appropriate staff consult with related committee(s) as needed
  • If moving forward: ED notifies President, Presidential Line, or entire Executive Committee as appropriate
  • Drafting: Sr Dir of Comms and Dir of Advocacy draft the statement
  • Review: Draft circulated to President/Presidential Line/Executive Committee for final input
  • Sign-off: ED reviews and signs off before posting
Unclear → Stop and route for approval
Authorized → Continue
Step 8
Has stakeholder impact been assessed?
Ask yourself:
  • Who is helped, harmed, included, or excluded?
  • Are trainees, patients, educators, underrepresented groups, or international members affected differently?
  • Could the statement create legal, funding, or partnership risks?
Note:
  • This should not veto a necessary statement — it should shape wording and scope.
Step 9
Can the society defend and follow through?
Ask yourself:
  • Can leaders answer press and member questions?
  • Is there a Q&A, evidence memo, and spokesperson plan?
  • Is there willingness to take criticism and sustain the position?
Not ready → Issue narrower statement or wait
Yes → Issue the statement
